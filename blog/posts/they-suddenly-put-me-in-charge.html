“They suddenly put me in charge of all sales. What now?”

(and the part nobody tells you: none of us were born with the skills we pretend to have)

The strangest promotion of my life happened on a Tuesday morning with the emotional subtlety of a car crash. I walked into the office planning to answer emails. By noon, my manager quit. By 12:07 I was “Head of Sales”.

No training. No ceremony. Just a Slack message from the CEO saying, “We need continuity. You’re the strongest one we have.”

There’s this myth in business that leaders appear fully formed. But the truth is uglier and lonelier: almost every leader is promoted before they’re ready. Most of us learned leadership the way people learn to swim after falling into a cold lake.

My first week was a carnival of bad decisions. I rewrote messaging I didn’t understand. I redesigned the pitch deck three times. I was scrambling.

It turns out leadership isn’t a show of strength. It’s the ability to say: “I don’t have the answer yet, but I’m here, I’m paying attention, and I won’t bullshit you.”

The turning point came when a junior rep told me he felt stupid for not “naturally knowing” how to close big deals. I told him: “Nobody naturally knows. The only difference between you and the ‘naturals’ is that they’ve failed more times than you’ve tried.”

What made the difference wasn’t brilliance. It was one shift: I stopped trying to be someone who was born ready, and started being someone who was willing to learn publicly.

If someone suddenly puts you in charge of sales — or anything, really — and you feel like an imposter, remember this: The people who seem confident weren’t born that way. They just got comfortable walking into rooms they didn’t feel qualified to be in.

And now it’s your turn.
