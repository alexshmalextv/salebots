Talking to CTOs and engineers when you’re a salesperson who isn’t technical

There’s a special kind of humiliation you only feel when you’re on a call with engineers and you realize, mid-sentence, that you’re confidently explaining something you barely understand. It’s not anger they give you in return — it’s the calm, quiet disappointment of someone watching a person drown slowly in a shallow pool.

I’ve lived that moment enough times to develop something like PTSD around certain technical phrases. You hear acronyms. They hear architecture. You hear “feature”. They hear “failure mode”.

Early in my career, I tried to fake it. I memorized buzzwords. One time I tried to explain “data consistency guarantees” to an engineer. He listened politely until I finished, then said, “Yeah, that’s… not how any of this works.” The rest of the call died like a candle in the wind.

The Turning Point

Years later, I realized something obvious: Engineers don’t expect you to understand the system. They expect you to understand the cost of being wrong.

The turning point came during a call with a CTO who looked like he hadn’t slept in three days. He said, “Look, I don’t need another salesperson trying to impress me. I need someone who won’t create more work for my team.”

I admitted — bluntly — that I wasn’t technical. Just: “I’m not technical and I won’t pretend. Tell me how you think, what you fear, what you don’t want me to screw up, and I’ll work around you.”

The whole call shifted. This wasn’t a technology conversation. It was a therapy session disguised as a demo.

If you walk in trying to impress them, you lose. If you walk in trying to understand their world, they’ll give you the blueprint.

But here’s the twist: understanding their world doesn’t mean being technical. It means being able to say “I don’t know, but I’ll get you the truth” and then actually doing it.

We won that deal. Not because our tech was perfect. But because they trusted that when something eventually broke (and something always breaks), we’d show up without lies.

So here’s the part that younger reps never believe until they live it: Selling to engineers is the easiest kind of selling. But only after you stop performing.

If you’re honest, thoughtful, and unafraid to expose what you don’t know, they’ll open the door. If you fake competence, they will close it so gently you won’t even realize they locked it.
